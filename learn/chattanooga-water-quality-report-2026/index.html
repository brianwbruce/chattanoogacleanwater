What's in Chattanooga's Water? 2026 Water Quality Report Breakdown

April 5, 2026

Every year, Chattanooga's water utility publishes a Consumer Confidence Report (CCR) detailing what's in the city's drinking water. It's public information, but let's be honest — most people don't read it. And if they do, the technical jargon makes it hard to understand what any of it actually means.

Here's a plain-English guide to understanding what's in your Chattanooga water and why it matters.

Where Chattanooga's Water Comes From

Chattanooga's drinking water is drawn from the Tennessee River near the Moccasin Bend area. The river collects runoff from agricultural land, industrial areas, and urban development upstream. The water treatment plant uses a multi-step process including coagulation, sedimentation, filtration, and disinfection to make the water safe for consumption.

The key word there is "safe" — as defined by EPA regulatory limits. But what does that actually look like in terms of what's in your glass?

Key Things Found in Chattanooga Water

Disinfection Byproducts

When chlorine is used to disinfect water, it reacts with organic matter in the source water and creates byproducts like trihalomethanes (TTHMs) and haloacetic acids (HAA5). These are monitored and regulated, but they're consistently detected in Chattanooga's water. They're one of the primary reasons many homeowners choose additional filtration at home.

Chlorine and Chloramines

These are intentionally added to kill bacteria during treatment and to maintain disinfection as water travels through the distribution system to your home. They're effective at their job, but they're also what gives tap water that pool-like taste and smell. The levels are within legal limits, but many people prefer water without them.

Turbidity

This measures the cloudiness of the water, which indicates the presence of suspended particles. While Chattanooga's treated water generally has low turbidity readings, any particles present can interfere with the disinfection process and potentially harbor microorganisms.

Lead and Copper

These metals don't come from the source water — they leach into the water from aging pipes and plumbing fixtures in the distribution system and in your home. Older homes in Chattanooga are at higher risk. Even low levels of lead exposure are concerning, especially for children and pregnant women.

Nitrates

Typically originating from agricultural runoff and natural deposits, nitrates are regularly monitored. While levels in Chattanooga have historically been below the maximum contaminant level, they're still present and are particularly concerning for infants.

What "Below Legal Limits" Really Means

When the water quality report says a contaminant is "below the maximum contaminant level," it means the utility is in compliance with federal regulations. That's good — it means they're not violating the law.

But here's the nuance: the EPA sets these limits as a balance between health protection and what's economically and technically feasible for water systems to achieve. Some health organizations and environmental groups advocate for much stricter limits than what's currently required by law.

Additionally, the report reflects system-wide averages and samples taken at specific points in the distribution network. Your individual home may have different levels depending on your location, the age of your plumbing, and other factors unique to your property.

The Gap Between Your Tap and the Treatment Plant

One of the most important things to understand is that water quality can change between the treatment plant and your faucet. The city tests water at the plant and at various points throughout the system, but they're not testing at your kitchen sink.

Factors that affect your specific water quality include:

  • The age and material of pipes in your neighborhood
  • The age and condition of your home's plumbing
  • Whether your home has a lead service line
  • How far you are from the treatment plant
  • Seasonal variations in source water quality

What You Can Do

Reading the annual report is a great first step — it shows you're paying attention to what your family is consuming. The next step is finding out what's happening at your specific address. A professional in-home water test gives you a clear picture of exactly what's coming out of your faucets, not just what's leaving the treatment plant.

From there, you can make an informed decision about whether additional treatment makes sense for your home.
